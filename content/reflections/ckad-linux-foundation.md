While I was working with Kubernetes on a daily basis, I faced challenges in understanding how to optimize a deployment configuration to reduce cost, but still ensure the application is stable. Even though I had knowledge in Docker and containerization, it wasn't sufficient for me to confidently make changes or debug the deployment configuration. I would **take hours trying to figure out why a pod couldn't start up**, even though there was no explicit error in the pipelines. I searched for logs or events in resource quotas, pod autoscalers, replicasets, probes, deployments, and pods, and would eventually find the root cause. However, this took a lot of time and effort (almost 1-2 hours) to look through them one by one, checking each container configuration, then deployment configuration, so on and so forth.

I thought that was highly inefficient. Although I knew the basics of how to use Kubernetes for deployment, I was not proficient enough to resolve issues. Hence, I decided to take on the CKAD cert and get hands-on familiarity with it. It took me around 4 months to study for this examination, and I managed to certify myself. Now, I am confident in solving issues related to Kubernetes deployments, and even optimizing pod resources. Currently, I own 8 Kubernetes deployment pipelines and manage everything about them, from code to whitelisting to deployment configuration.

Through this, I came to understand the need for certification: we don't know what we don't know. By certifying ourselves, we become more aware of our weaknesses in the area, and work on improving them. Additionally, there is more content than we might realise. Before I certified myself, I was only aware of pods and deployments. Now, I am aware of much more than just these, and can confidently deploy applications with Kubernetes.
