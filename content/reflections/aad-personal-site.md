I have to admit, I am not the best frontend engineer. While I have fiddled with this area of development, my expertise still lies in backend development, data pipelines and data visualization. Could I have carved out time to learn and get better at this? Honestly, yes. But I had other priorities pulling at me, and the time never quite materialised. Notwithstanding, I am highly inspired by beautiful webpages and personal sites, and always wanted to block out time to just try building them. Maybe my goalpost was too far and intimidating, so I always hesitated to start. Then one day, while scrolling on the web, I saw posts on how AI can generate highly valuable webpages in just minutes. That was when I knew it was time to begin my journey to build webpages, with the help of AI. **I wanted to build not just a webpage. I wanted something unmistakably mine**.

This page started out as a resume website. I dumped my resume details and passed relevant content links to the agent, and watched it spit out the first version of my personal site: a generic, replicable webpage that anyone could recreate. Did I hate it? No. But I certainly didn't love it. Then I realised what I actually wanted was a personal site, not a resume replica. I didn't want another "source" for my resume. I wanted something that screams "This is Jermyn", something that reflects who I am. So I started researching how to improve the page, and went into prompt engineering to understand how to sharpen my prompts, since single-line prompts were not making the cut. Even after multiple iterations, I was still unsatisfied. So I restarted my own loop, went back to research, and leveraged open-source UI/UX skills and MCPs to critique and improve the page through prompting and personally validating the output. There will definitely be more to come, but for now, I am satisfied with what I have (in terms of UI/UX).

From this experience, I learnt a lot, and here is my key takeaway.

**AI without humans will produce consistency, but not personality.**

If we want something uniquely ours, we need to stay in the loop and steer the conversations. There were plenty of frustrations along the way, where the agent couldn't grasp exactly what I wanted, and I had to iterate many times before it got the vision right. The next time I enhance this site (from a UI/UX standpoint), I will prepare documents of the inspirations I have and add them to the context: showing the agent instead of telling it, which cuts down on iterations of fixing one button.

P.S. This is definitely an experience where the saying "easier said than done" holds true!
